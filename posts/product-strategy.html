Product Strategy

Let's walk through building a product strategy, from surfacing problems to taking actions that solve them. There are situations where it's appropriate to start with Understanding the Target Customer before Defining the Problem, depending on the context.

Strategy Inputs:

1) Define the Problem

If you walked up to someone on the street and asked, “What problem are you experiencing?” how would they respond? This is the problem statement we want to define. Perhaps something is inaccessible, too expensive, or takes too much time. Each product you work on helps solve a problem. What is that problem?

Problem statement examples:

  • - It's hard for me to access a doctor
  • - I can't eat a healthy meal each day
  • - I'm unable to remember all of my passwords
  • - It takes too long to complete my task
  • - I don't understand how to use this feature
  • - I can't find what I'm looking for

Getting to the Root Problem: what's really going on here?

Next, break the problem down to its root cause, something actionable. Do this by asking “Why?” until you reach the core issue. For example: "Why can't you eat a healthy meal each day?"

  • - I don't know how to cook → Why? I don't have access to beginner-friendly meals
  • - I think cooking takes too long → Why? I don't know any quick meals to make
  • - I don't know which meals to cook → Why? I don't know which meals align with my goals
  • - I think healthy ingredients are expensive → Why? I don't know which ingredients are affordable
  • - I don't enjoy the taste of healthy food → Why? I don't know which meals align with my taste
  • - I'm not motivated to cook → Why? I don't see immediate benefits from eating healthy

Gather Evidence: is this problem real?

Gather evidence to validate your assumptions. Strategy begins with a deep understanding of the problems your customers experience.

2) Understand the Target Customer: who has this problem and wants a solution?

It may sound counterintuitive, but instead of solving this problem for everyone, start by solving for a narrow user segment. Think of this user segment as a pond where the fish communicate with each other. The pond should be big enough for business impact, but small enough so ripples can reach every corner. Your goal is to have fish jumping into your boat as word of mouth spreads through the pond.

Understand the Context of the Target Customer Situation: what's really going on in their life?

The user segment should include like-minded people in similar contextual situations. What is going on in their life? What situation is driving them to want to solve this problem?

Their Goal: what are they trying to do?

The user segment should focus on customers with a common goal. Learn this by asking questions until you reach the goal. For example: "Why do you want to eat a healthy meal each day?"

  • - I want to live a healthier lifestyle → Why? I've received a health diagnosis and I want to lower my cholesterol.
  • - I want to live a healthier lifestyle → Why? I'm starting a new workout routine and I want to build muscle.
  • - I want to live a healthier lifestyle → Why? I'm uncomfortable with my appearance and I want to lose weight.

Let's focus on the user segment whose goal is to build muscle. This is our pond.

Their Hiring Criteria: what do they need in a product?

What should you account for when building a product for this user segment?

  • - I need a product that helps me prepare meals
  • - I need a product that simplifies cooking
  • - I need a product that shows the nutrient breakdown of my meal
  • - I need a product that suggests recipes tailored to my goals

Their Firing Criteria: why would they stop using a product?

What should you avoid when building a product for this user segment?

  • - I don't want a product that makes cooking a chore
  • - I don't want a product that is complex
  • - I don't want a product that is high effort
  • - I don't want a product that suggests meals misaligned with my goals

Creating Personas

Personas help humanize the target user segment by personifying the context we've learned. You're fishing in a pond of these personas. These are the fish to catch:

  • Gym-Goer Gabe is starting a workout routine and needs a simple way to find and prepare healthy meals tailored to his fitness goal of building muscle.

3) Identify Competitors: who solves this problem?

What products enable your target customer to eat healthy to build muscle? Your competition includes everyone that solves this problem. Where do you fit in this market? For example:

  • - MyFitnessPal enables customers to eat healthy to build muscle by suggesting recipes and showing nutrient breakdowns
  • - HelloFresh enables customers to eat healthy to build muscle by suggesting recipes and having ingredients delivered to their home
  • - Whole Foods enables customers to eat healthy to build muscle by offering pre-made meals
  • - Favor enables customers to eat healthy to build muscle by having pre-made meal delivered to their home

Learn the direction competitors are moving in by staying up-to-date with release notes. Consider how you might differentiate yourself from competitors when prioritizing strategic actions to take. Use hiring and firing criteria as guiding policies.

4) Recognize the Business Goal: what are you trying to do?

Output Metrics: the roads that lead to revenue

Your business has a goal, just like your user segment. Output metrics lead to revenue, the actions you take should impact output metrics.

  • - Increase trial signups
  • - Increase bill-throughs
  • - Reduce account cancellations

Input Metrics: focusing on the "Aha!" moment, the key action signaling value was received

Often called the “North Star” metric. This key action, when moved, will also move your output metrics. The action represents when a user receives product value. It is the action you want users to take when first using your product. Choose a value representing action that creates network effects and/or switching costs.

  • - Recipe dwell time >= est. cook time
  • - Recipe saves

5) Set the Guiding Policy: where should you focus your effort?

You need to decide where to focus your effort before you decide what to build. This decision depends on whether your product has “Product Market Fit” (PMF) or not.

  • - If you're in the Pre-PMF stage, focus efforts developing a strong counter-position against the competition.
  • - If you have PMF, focus efforts on building network effects, increasing switching costs, or creating scale economies.
  • - If you're a market leader, focus your efforts on branding, cornered resources, or process power.

Strategy Outputs:

6) Choose the Root Problems to Solve Now

By now you understand your target user segment, context about their situation, their goal to achieve and the problems in their way, the competitive solutions that solve those problems, the goal of your business, and where to focus your effort. Now decide which problems that, when solved, will help users achieve their goal and the business achieve its goal.

From the surfaced problems, let's focus on:

  • - I don't have access to beginner-friendly meals
  • - I don't know any quick meals to make
  • - I don't know which meals align with my goals

Keep focus by solving a few problems at a time.

7) Paint your Vision

What does the world look like after solving these problems? A vision paints a picture of the destination you're trying to reach so your team and customers know what direction the product is moving in.

  • A future where preparing the healthy meal you need will feel as effortless as making a cup of coffee.

Evangelize your vision. Share your vision. Communicate your vision. A tactic I use is changing my video meeting background to include my vision's text so everyone I meet with will know it.

8) Determine your Actions: what are we going to build now?

We know where we're headed, but how are we going to get there? You will want to work with your team to define which actions to take (sometimes called “initiatives”). What is built should be found at the intersection of customer-centric actions (solving user problems) and technology-led actions (using your tech stack). Each action should impact input metrics that move output metrics. Additionally, actions should factor in your user segments' hiring and firing criteria and, if possible, differentiate your product from competitors.

Inflections: what new thing has been introduced?

What "new thing" was introduced? How can that “new thing” empower your product? Inflections come from technological advances, regulatory changes, or belief shifts. Inflections are waves of change in an industry or population. For example:

  • - Technology: LLMs, smartphones, cloud computing
  • - Regulation: telemedicine laws from covid, GDPR, cannabis legalization
  • - Belief: work from home adoption, climate change awareness, crypotcurrency as a financial asset

Use Cases: how will the user segment use your product to achieve their goal?

Consider the use cases. How will your user segment use your product to achieve their goal? For example:

  • - Gym-Goer Gabe can discover new, simple recipes limited to the ingredients in his kitchen on our product to eat healthy meals each day.
  • - Gym-Goer Gabe can access nutrient breakdowns of each recipe on our product to eat health meals and build muscle.

Brainstorm Ideas: how might we solve these problems?

Work with your team to surface ideas for what to build next. Start by asking this question: “Using the information we know, how might we {solve this problem}?”

  • - How might we provide access to beginner-friendly meals?
  • - How might we provide access to quick meals?
  • - How might we provide access to meals that align with user goals?
  • - [Bonus] How might we solve these problems in a never-before-seen way?

List out all of the brainstorm ideas for each problem. For example, to provide access to meals that align with user goals:

  • - LLM-Powered Meal Alignment Tool: input fitness goals and dietary preferences, generate personalized meal plans with nutrient breakdowns.
  • - Goal-Driven Meal Tags: tag recipes on our platform (e.g: high protein, low fat, calorie dense).
  • - Dynamic Meal Generator: create recipes tailored to specific goals using available ingredients.
  • - AI-Powered Meal Substitutions: suggest healthy substitutions to make favorite meals align with fitness goals.

Validate Ideas: can we build this?

Validate your assumptions on if the proposed idea is feasible and viable.

Prioritize Ideas: which ideas are most impactful?

Prioritization is a balance of reach, impact, and effort:

  • - Reach: how many est. users will experience this benefit?
  • - Impact: is this a big bet, an improvement, or nice to have?
  • - Effort: how much est. time will this take to build?

Often, prioritization comes from your product sense.

Prioritize Counter-Positioning: playing offense

How can your ideas position yourself against the competition? Emphasize hiring criteria, firing criteria, and competitive differentiation in your projects. Consider how you might position your product:

  • For Gym-Goer Gabes who are dissatisfied with complex meal tracking apps, our product is a simple meal planning subscription suggesting recipes tailored to your fitness goals. Unlike MyFitnessPal, we have developed a product that uses ingredients available in your home to suggest easy-to-make meals that help you reach your fitness goals.

9) Execute your Strategy: start building!

Gather requirements, provide context, get commitments, assemble resources, and build valuable products with your team.
